Basic Watershed Characteristics

The Fox River drains 2,186 square miles, 924 square miles in Wisconsin and 1,252 square miles in Illinois. Within Wisconsin, the River flows 84 miles from its headwaters in extreme southern Washington County, through eastern Waukesha County, and through western Racine and Kenosha Counties. The Fox River and many of its larger tributaries are Federal Clean Water Act Section 303(d) listed impaired waters. Such streams commonly have degraded biological communities, high turbidity, and low dissolved oxygen; conditions largely attributed to excessive soil and nutrient loads. As such, action must be taken to improve water quality.

Despite the River’s compromised water quality, the Fox River watershed is a uniquely valuable resource to southeastern Wisconsin. In Wisconsin alone, the watershed includes about 750 miles of perennial streams and nearly 80 named lakes of which 38 are lakes considered to be “major lakes” with a surface area greater than 50 acres. Hence, powerboating, paddle sports, and fishing are particularly popular in the watershed. This area also contains abundant county and city parks, recreational trails, and numerous wildlife areas. Such areas harbor at least 23 endangered, 26 threatened and 70 special concern plant and animal species, and 30 rare aquatic and terrestrial communities.


Non-Point Source Nutrient and Sediment Loads

Non-Point Source Nutrient and Sediment Loads Data collected over the past 20 years throughout the Wisconsin portion of the watershed demonstrate that, in most instances, River water phosphorus concentrations exceed WDNR water quality standards. However, phosphorus concentration patterns are complex. Phosphorus concentrations have increased at some sites and have decreased at others. Continued monitoring is essential for tracking progress towards improving water quality.
